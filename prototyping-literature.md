# Prototyping in Digital Service Design: Literature Reference Table

This collection maps the intellectual landscape of prototyping across two traditions — the design-exploratory and the hypothesis-driven. It covers foundational theory, the different purposes prototypes serve (exploration, evaluation, communication, technical feasibility), digital materiality, service prototyping, and the behavioral economics and experimentation frameworks that underpin hypothesis-driven approaches. It supports a course module for design students working with digital services.

*Compiled: March 2026*

---

## Foundational Theory: What Prototypes Are and Do

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [What Do Prototypes Prototype?](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/bychapter/shortpapers/6-houde.pdf) | Houde & Hill | 1997 | Book chapter | The canonical framework for prototyping: prototypes explore "role" (what it could do for a user), "look and feel" (sensory experience), and "implementation" (how it works technically). Essential foundation for understanding that no single prototype addresses everything. |
| [Sketching User Experiences: Getting the Design Right and the Right Design](https://www.worldcat.org/title/sketching-user-experiences-getting-the-design-right-and-the-right-design/oclc/141381tried) | Buxton, Bill | 2007 | Book | Distinguishes sketching from prototyping — sketches are for exploration (suggest, explore, question), while prototypes are for evaluation (describe, refine, answer). Foundational for understanding that different fidelity levels serve different design purposes. |
| [Prototyping: A Practitioner's Guide](https://www.worldcat.org/title/prototyping-a-practitioners-guide/oclc/427483321) | Warfel, Todd Zaki | 2009 | Book | Practitioner-oriented guide covering the full spectrum from paper to interactive prototypes. Useful for its practical taxonomy of prototype types and when to use each. |
| [The Role of Design Artifacts in Design Thinking](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-319-06823-7_11) | Dalsgaard, Peter | 2014 | Book chapter | Extends the conversation about what design artifacts (including prototypes) actually accomplish in the design process — they are not just representations but active instruments of inquiry. |
| [The Anatomy of Prototypes](https://doi.org/10.1145/1551788.1551806) | Lim, Stolterman & Tenenberg | 2008 | Journal article (ACM TOCHI) | Proposes that prototypes work through "filtering" — selectively manifesting some qualities while deliberately leaving others unresolved. Offers a principled vocabulary for deciding what a prototype should and should not include. |

## Prototyping as Exploration and Inquiry

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Research Through Design as a Method for Interaction Design Research in HCI](https://doi.org/10.1145/1240624.1240704) | Zimmerman, Forlizzi & Evenson | 2007 | Conference paper (CHI) | Establishes prototyping (as "research through design") as a legitimate research method. Making things is a way of producing knowledge, not just a precursor to evaluation. Critical for framing explorative prototyping. |
| [Constructive Design Research](https://www.worldcat.org/title/constructive-design-research/oclc/794365892) | Koskinen, Zimmerman, Binder, Redström & Wensveen | 2011 | Book | Maps three traditions of design research where making is central: Lab, Field, and Showroom. Positions prototyping as knowledge production rather than mere product development. |
| [Design Things and Design Thinking: Contemporary Participatory Design Challenges](https://doi.org/10.1162/desi.2011.27.3.101) | Binder, De Michelis, Ehn, Jacucci, Linde & Wagner | 2011 | Journal article (Design Issues) | Introduces "design things" as socio-material assemblies. Prototypes are not just artifacts but things-to-think-with and things-to-negotiate-with in participatory settings. |
| [Concept-Driven Interaction Design Research](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10209-012-0273-9) | Stolterman & Wiberg | 2010 | Journal article (Universal Access in the Information Society) | Proposes that interaction design research can be driven by theoretical concepts manifested through prototypes. The prototype becomes a way to operationalize and test theoretical ideas. |
| [Making Design Theory](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262036658/making-design-theory/) | Redström, Johan | 2017 | Book | Argues that design programs (frameworks of intent) and design experiments (prototypes, artifacts) together constitute design theory. Prototyping is not just applied theory — it generates theory. |

## The Design Space: Viability, Feasibility, Desirability

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Change by Design](https://www.worldcat.org/title/change-by-design-how-design-thinking-transforms-organizations-and-inspires-innovation/oclc/310400227) | Brown, Tim | 2009 | Book | Popularized the viability–feasibility–desirability Venn diagram as the "sweet spot" for innovation. Prototypes in this framing serve to test all three dimensions, ideally cheaply and early. |
| [Sprint: How to Solve Big Problems and Test New Ideas in Just Five Days](https://www.worldcat.org/title/sprint/oclc/908083430) | Knapp, Zeratsky & Kowitz | 2016 | Book | Codifies a structured five-day prototyping process used at Google Ventures. Demonstrates how to build "just enough" prototypes to learn from users, focusing heavily on desirability testing. |
| [The Lean Startup](https://www.worldcat.org/title/lean-startup/oclc/712038496) | Ries, Eric | 2011 | Book | Introduced the "minimum viable product" (MVP) as a prototype for testing business viability. Influential but often misapplied — conflates prototyping with product launching. Useful as a reference point for what viability-focused prototyping looks like. |
| [Value Proposition Design](https://www.worldcat.org/title/value-proposition-design/oclc/889049498) | Osterwalder, Pigneur, Bernarda & Smith | 2014 | Book | Connects prototyping to business model testing — prototypes as experiments to validate or invalidate value propositions. Provides a structured approach to viability prototyping. |
| [Pretotype It](https://www.pretotyping.org/pretotype-it) | Savoia, Alberto | 2011 | Book / Blog | Coins "pretotyping" — testing whether anyone would want a product before building it. Complements Ries's MVP with even lighter-weight approaches focused on desirability and viability. |

## Digital Materiality and Material Exploration

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Computational Composites](https://doi.org/10.1145/1314161.1314215) | Vallgårda & Redström | 2007 | Conference paper (CHI) | Proposes that digital technology should be understood as a design material with its own properties. Prototyping becomes a way to explore what a computational material can do, analogous to a sculptor working with clay. |
| [Interaction Design and the Concept of 'Material'](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/289722682) | Wiberg, Mikael | 2014 | Journal article (interactions) | Argues for treating digital technology as material in design practice. Prototyping-as-material-exploration means working with code, data, and connectivity to discover their affordances. |
| [Giving Form to Computational Things](https://research.chalmers.se/publication/174tried) | Vallgårda, Anna | 2014 | PhD dissertation (Chalmers) | Develops a framework for understanding how designers give form to computational things through prototyping — temporal, spatial, and behavioral form are all designable through iterative making. |
| [Programming as Design Practice](https://doi.org/10.1145/2851581.2892576) | Löwgren, Jonas | 2016 | Conference paper (CHI Extended Abstracts) | Positions programming as a legitimate design practice, not merely implementation. When designers code, they explore the material possibilities of software. |
| [Designing Connected Products](https://www.worldcat.org/title/designing-connected-products/oclc/969431043) | Rowland, Goodman, Charlier, Light & Lui | 2015 | Book | Practical guide to prototyping IoT and connected products where the "material" includes networks, APIs, sensors. Demonstrates how technical material exploration feeds into design decisions. |

## Fidelity, Roles, and Prototype Strategy

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Paper Prototyping](https://www.worldcat.org/title/paper-prototyping/oclc/51983258) | Snyder, Carolyn | 2003 | Book | Definitive guide to low-fidelity prototyping. Demonstrates that you can learn enormous amounts from the simplest possible prototypes — provided you're clear about what you're testing. |
| [Prototyping for Tiny Fingers](https://doi.org/10.1145/175276.175288) | Rettig, Marc | 1994 | Journal article (Communications of the ACM) | Early and influential argument for paper prototyping in interface design. Emphasizes speed, accessibility, and the importance of testing interaction rather than visual polish. |
| [Experience Prototyping](https://doi.org/10.1145/347642.347802) | Buchenau & Suri | 2000 | Conference paper (DIS) | Introduces "experience prototyping" — prototyping the subjective experience of using a service or product, not just the interface. Foundational for service design prototyping. |
| [The Wizard of Oz](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/book/the-encyclopedia-of-human-computer-interaction-2nd-ed/wizard-of-oz-prototyping) | Dahlbäck, Jönsson & Ahrenberg | 1993 | Journal article | Establishes "Wizard of Oz" as a prototyping technique — simulating system behavior with a hidden human operator. Essential for prototyping AI, voice interfaces, and complex backend behavior. |
| [Should I Use a High or Low Fidelity Prototype?](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-prototype-hi-lo-fidelity/) | Pernice, Kara | 2016 | Blog post (Nielsen Norman Group) | Practitioner-oriented decision framework for choosing prototype fidelity based on what you need to learn. Useful starting point for students navigating fidelity choices. |
| [The Right Tool for the Job: Five Collaborative Prototyping Strategies](https://medium.com/@jsaito/the-right-tool-for-the-job-five-collaborative-prototyping-strategies-b33b4d7ee1a7) | Saito, Jason | 2015 | Blog post | Maps out five prototyping strategies by goal: communicate, explore, evaluate, learn, and sell. Practical framework for matching prototype type to intent. |

## Service Design and Service Prototyping

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [This is Service Design Doing](https://www.worldcat.org/title/this-is-service-design-doing/oclc/1004773741) | Stickdorn, Hormess, Lawrence & Schneider | 2018 | Book | Comprehensive service design handbook with extensive coverage of service prototyping — from desktop walkthroughs to live service pilots. ## The Hypothesis-Driven Tradition: Experimentation and Validated Learning

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Lean UX: Designing Great Products with Agile Teams](https://www.worldcat.org/title/lean-ux/oclc/1241058076) | Gothelf, Jeff & Seiden, Josh | 2021 (3rd ed.) | Book | Integrates hypothesis-driven prototyping into agile development. Every design decision is framed as a hypothesis to be tested with the minimum viable experiment. The bridge text between Lean Startup and UX practice. |
| [Experimentation Works: The Surprising Power of Business Experiments](https://www.worldcat.org/title/experimentation-works/oclc/1139217636) | Thomke, Stefan | 2020 | Book | Documents how organizations like Booking.com, Amazon, and Microsoft run thousands of experiments simultaneously. Positions A/B testing and controlled experiments as the primary form of prototyping at scale. |
| [Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments: A Practical Guide to A/B Testing](https://www.worldcat.org/title/trustworthy-online-controlled-experiments/oclc/1117711066) | Kohavi, Ron; Tang, Diane & Xu, Ya | 2020 | Book | The definitive technical reference for running A/B tests at scale. Covers statistical methodology, pitfalls, and organizational practices. Represents the most rigorous form of hypothesis-driven prototyping. |
| [Competing Against Luck: The Story of Innovation and Customer Choice](https://www.worldcat.org/title/competing-against-luck/oclc/953716413) | Christensen, Clayton; Hall, Taddy; Dillon, Karen & Duncan, David | 2016 | Book | Introduces "Jobs to Be Done" theory — customers don't buy products, they hire them to do a job. Reframes prototyping around testing whether your product does the job better than alternatives. |

## Behavioral Economics: The User as Decision-Maker

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Thinking, Fast and Slow](https://www.worldcat.org/title/thinking-fast-and-slow/oclc/706020998) | Kahneman, Daniel | 2011 | Book | The foundational text on dual-process theory (System 1 / System 2). Most user decisions are fast, automatic, and biased — not rational and deliberate. Essential background for understanding why hypothesis-driven prototyping focuses on observed behavior, not stated preferences. |
| [Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness](https://www.worldcat.org/title/nudge/oclc/191930763) | Thaler, Richard & Sunstein, Cass | 2008 | Book | Introduces "choice architecture" — the way options are presented changes what people choose. Prototyping in this tradition means testing which presentation of choices produces the desired behavioral outcome. Raises ethical questions about designing for persuasion vs. autonomy. |
| [Predictably Irrational: The Hidden Forces That Shape Our Decisions](https://www.worldcat.org/title/predictably-irrational/oclc/182521026) | Ariely, Dan | 2008 | Book | Demonstrates through experiments that human irrationality is systematic and predictable. Provides the empirical basis for designing around cognitive biases — anchoring, loss aversion, the decoy effect. |
| [Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion](https://www.worldcat.org/title/influence/oclc/63096950) | Cialdini, Robert | 2006 (revised ed.) | Book | Identifies six principles of persuasion: reciprocity, commitment, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity. Widely applied in conversion optimization and growth design — the behavioral toolkit behind many hypothesis-driven prototypes. |
| [Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products](https://www.worldcat.org/title/hooked/oclc/878381tried) | Eyal, Nir | 2014 | Book | Models how products create user habits through trigger-action-reward-investment loops. Represents the most optimization-focused end of behavioral design — prototyping for engagement and retention. Raises questions about ethical boundaries. |
| [Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics](https://www.worldcat.org/title/misbehaving/oclc/890553tried) | Thaler, Richard | 2015 | Book | The intellectual history of behavioral economics as a field. Useful for understanding why this tradition exists and what it was reacting against (the "rational agent" model of classical economics). Contextualizes the assumptions behind hypothesis-driven prototyping. |

## Contemporary Perspectives: AI, Ethics, and Speculative Prototyping

| Source | Author(s) | Year | Type | Key Contribution |
|--------|-----------|------|------|-----------------|
| [Speculative Everything](https://www.worldcat.org/title/speculative-everything/oclc/858976258) | Dunne & Raby | 2013 | Book | Positions prototyping as speculation — making artifacts that provoke discussion about possible futures rather than solving immediate problems. Essential counterpoint to purely instrumental views of prototyping. |
| [Design Fiction: A Short Essay on Design, Science, Fact and Fiction](https://blog.nearfuturelaboratory.com/2009/03/17/design-fiction-a-short-essay-on-design-science-fact-and-fiction/) | Sterling, Bruce | 2009 | Blog post / Essay | Coins "design fiction" — using prototypes and designed artifacts to tell stories about possible futures. Complementary to Dunne & Raby, more accessible and practitioner-oriented. |
| [More-Than-Human Design and AI](https://doi.org/10.1145/3544549.3585609) | Giaccardi & Redström | 2020 | Conference paper | Explores what happens to prototyping when the "material" includes AI and machine learning — systems that learn and change. Challenges traditional prototype-as-snapshot approaches. |
| [Ethical Exploration: Towards a Working Method for Responsible Innovation](https://www.nesta.org.uk/) | Various | 2020+ | Toolkit / Guide | Represents the growing movement to use prototyping as ethical inquiry — building to understand consequences, not just functionality. Prototypes as instruments of responsible innovation. |

## Key Concepts Cross-Reference

| Concept | Source(s) | Relevance to Argument |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Role–Look&Feel–Implementation triangle | Houde & Hill (1997) | Foundation: every prototype foregrounds some dimensions and backgrounds others |
| Sketch vs. Prototype distinction | Buxton (2007) | Establishes that exploration and evaluation require different artifacts |
| Filtering dimensions | Lim, Stolterman & Tenenberg (2008) | Principled framework for deciding what to include/exclude in a prototype |
| Viability–Feasibility–Desirability | Brown (2009), Ries (2011), Osterwalder et al. (2014) | The classic "sweet spot" model — each prototype tests a different mix of these |
| Research through Design | Zimmerman et al. (2007), Koskinen et al. (2011), Redström (2017) | Prototyping as knowledge production, not just product development |
| Digital materiality | Vallgårda & Redström (2007), Wiberg (2014), Löwgren (2016) | Code, data, and connectivity as design materials to be explored through making |
| Experience prototyping | Buchenau & Suri (2000) | Prototyping subjective experience, not just interface — critical for services |
| Fidelity spectrum | Snyder (2003), Rettig (1994), Pernice (2016) | Different fidelity levels answer different questions — match fidelity to learning goal |
| Speculative / critical prototyping | Dunne & Raby (2013), Sterling (2009) | Prototypes as provocations about possible futures, not just solutions |
| Service prototyping | Blomkvist (2014), Stickdorn et al. (2018), Polaine et al. (2013) | Extending prototyping from products to services — time, touchpoints, journeys |
| Explorative vs. normative | Redström (2017), Stolterman & Wiberg (2010) | Prototypes can open up the design space (explorative) or converge toward a solution (normative) |
| Build–Measure–Learn | Ries (2011), Gothelf & Seiden (2021) | The hypothesis-driven loop: every prototype is an experiment with a predicted outcome |
| System 1 / System 2 | Kahneman (2011), Ariely (2008) | Most user decisions are fast and biased — prototyping should test observed behavior, not stated preferences |
| Choice architecture & nudges | Thaler & Sunstein (2008), Cialdini (2006) | How options are presented changes what people choose — prototyping as behavioral experiment |
| A/B testing at scale | Kohavi et al. (2020), Thomke (2020) | The most rigorous form of hypothesis-driven prototyping — thousands of simultaneous experiments |
| Jobs to Be Done | Christensen et al. (2016) | Reframes prototyping around whether a product does the "job" better than alternatives |
| Habit formation | Eyal (2014) | Prototyping for engagement and retention — and the ethical questions this raises |
| Interpretation vs. measurement | Across both traditions | The epistemological divide: qualitative insight vs. statistical evidence as forms of learning |
